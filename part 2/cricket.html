For this part of the lab I chose to model a cricket sound. I mostly followed the description in the book, but I had a lot of trouble making the cricket noise sound less digital.

My cricket noise can be broken down into 3 parts: the cricket frequencies, the phasor, and the AM synthesis. The cricket frequencies are variables fund, part, and low. The fundamental and partial frequencies are the main high pitched cricket noise. These I found in the book, however I decided to go with a fundamental frequency of 4000 Hz instead of 4500 like the book says because that sounded more realistic. However, the 4500 Hz sounds best when listened through headphones. Next, I chose to add a low frequency (not in book) because when I looked up cricket noises they all seemed to have this very low pitched hum to them. The low frequency is best heard through headphones, and it models the muffled friction caused by the cricket rubbing its legs against its wings. The phasor is also from the book and controls the rate at which the cricket chirps. Finally, the AM synthesis applied to the frequencies models how close the ridges on the crickets wings are.

While programming, I found that the cricket sounded too digital. To fix this I tried to add an LPF to muffle some of the high frequencies and muddle the sound as crickets are usually under leaves. This did not work. I then tried an all pass filter to displace the frequencies and cause a phasing effect. This did not work either, so I tried to add some spacialization with delay. The delay, however, made the crickets sound weird due to phase cancelation. I tried a bunch of frequencies, but didn't like any. So, I finally settled on adding the unfiltered brown noise over top. This was meant to make the listener feel like they were outside with a cricket around them.

My biggest struggle came right before submitting. I wanted to show someone my cricket, and played the audio out loud. It sounded horrible. The whole time I had been coding with noise cancelling headphones. When I played my sound out loud, the cricket was way too high-pitched and the low frequency was lost. For this, I lowered the frequency and the global gain. This helped.
